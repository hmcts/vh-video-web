Accessibility statement for the HMCTS video hearing service

This service allows all participants to take part in a court hearing by video - from their home, office or somewhere else using a laptop or desktop computer.

This accessibility statement is based on evaluation conducted by the Digital Accessibility Centre (DAC), Neath, during September 2019.

This website is run by HM Courts and Tribunals Service. We want as many people as possible to be able to use this service. For example, this means you should be able to:

  • change colours, contrast levels and fonts
  • zoom in up to 300% without the text spilling off the screen
  • navigate most of the website using just a keyboard
  • navigate most of the website using speech recognition software
  • listen to most of the website using a screen reader

We’ve also made the website text as simple as possible to understand.

AbilityNet has advice on making your device easier to use if you have a disability.

How accessible is this website?

Fully video hearings provide an additional channel for conducting a hearing. While they are being made as accessible as possible, they may not be suitable for everyone.

The video hearings service includes a triage process so that people can make the court aware of any particular condition or impairment that may impact their ability to take part in a video hearing.

It will always be for a judge to decide whether to use the option of a fully video hearing, and to ensure the interests of justice are served in any given hearing.

During testing by DAC, users with cognitive impairment and dyslexia were able to navigate the website ‘completely independently, with no issues’. The access issues reported particularly affected those who are visually impaired or use voice activation technology.

What to do if you cannot access parts of this website

If you need help to access the information on this website, contact the video hearings team:

  • email video-hearings@justice.gov.uk
  • call 0300 303 0655

Available between 9am and 5pm, Monday to Friday.

Reporting accessibility problems with this website

We’re always looking to improve the accessibility of this website. If you find any problems not listed on this page or think we’re not meeting accessibility requirements, contact the video hearings team (above).

Enforcement procedure

The Equality and Human Rights Commission (EHRC) is responsible for enforcing the Public Sector Bodies (Websites and Mobile Applications) (No. 2) Accessibility Regulations 2018 (the ‘accessibility regulations’). If you’re not happy with how we respond to your complaint, contact the Equality Advisory and Support Service (EASS).

Technical information about this website’s accessibility

HM Courts and Tribunals Service is committed to making its website accessible, in accordance with the Public Sector Bodies (Websites and Mobile Applications) (No. 2) Accessibility Regulations 2018.

This website is partially compliant with the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines version 2.1 AA standard, due to the non-compliances listed below.

Non accessible content

A number of accessibility and usability issues were reported. These particularly impacted those who are visually impaired or use voice activation technology. Most issues were resolved before the private beta was released. Those that remain are shown below.

Non compliance with the accessibility regulations

Information videos

We currently use video to help users see what their experience would be like either attending a court building or taking part in a video hearing. This has proved popular in user testing and we’ll make the necessary changes to make the videos more accessible by the end of 2019.

Equipment check

We use video to help users test whether their computer equipment is suitable for a video hearing. This has caused some accessibility issues, detailed below.

We use a visual method to indicate to the user that their microphone is working. This is not suitable for those with visual impairments. We’re considering providing an audible indicator so they know their microphone is picking up sound.

The video intentionally has no controls. It needs to play to the end to detect the strength of the user’s internet connection. This takes at least 20 to 30 seconds. We’ve added an explanation.

By Spring 2020 we’ll review the way we help users test their equipment to address the accessibility issues identified in the DAC report.

Not within scope/not applicable

In preparing for a video hearing, potential participants are asked a series of questions. These are presented as one question per page, with the option of a ‘yes’ or ‘no’ answer. DAC advised that we should group the ‘yes’ and ‘no’ buttons to assist screen reader users. (WCAG reference 1.3.1 – A). This advice reflects guidance for longer forms with separate groups of questions on a single page, which is not applicable in this instance.

How we tested this website

The website was tested by the Digital Accessibility Centre, Neath, during September 2019.

This is an excerpt from their report explaining their methodology:

“The website was tested by a team of experienced auditors, many of who are disabled individuals and users of adaptive technology. The combination of subjective pan-disability user feedback and comprehensive technical auditing allows us to measure how the website performs technically and practically, thereby offering an essential added dimension to our test results that other methods of testing cannot provide.”

User testing:

“Manual accessibility checking was conducted by a team of disabled individuals, using a range of adaptive technologies (hardware and software designed to facilitate the use of computers by people with disabilities).”

Technical auditing:

“Technical auditing involves the experienced application of a number of technical auditing and standards compliance assessment tools. This combined with an extensive knowledge of WCAG, its application and wider global practice provides the DAC website with further credibility and quality.”
